What is a prototype?

An early model of something that can be replicated and improved upon
in successive versions.

Let's go back to products. A prototype is a basic version of a product, often created to test a particular aspect of that product. It's made to fail.

Test interactions and flows

Is this a solution that customers expect?

We designers are often eager to try out novel ways of solving problems, even when novelty isn't necessary and might even prove more challenging to users.

Test interactions and flows

Is the solution discoverable?

Even if the solution is novel, testing will help you find out if users will have trouble learning it or if it would be better to go with something more conventional.
This gives you opportunities to move from usable to the Delight!

Iterate quickly

Create quick iterations based on feedback

With positive feedback you can start fleshing out details

… and negative feedback won't make you nervous either

You won't be fazed by negative feedback because the prototype wasn't that hard to put together, and you're looking for problems. Better to get negative feedback from your team than an angry tweetstorm from customers.

Solve problems through rapid iteration

We don't know anything, us designers. No one does. But we can figure stuff out by starting out with an educated guess and testing.

Ship with confidence

“Ship it” —PM

PMs are not known for lacking confidence. They model and chart and put together documents, which is great. Except it becomes a problem when these documents are set in stone. A designer should ask lots of questions if all that the PM provided was a requirements doc and a timeline.

Your requirements doc
is not enough

No matter how much user research it's based on.

Great product managers understand this, and plan for prototyping and testing as part of the project scope beyond the initial research conducted.

Research. Prototype. Repeat.

User and market research is great and everyone should be doing it all the time. But it's not enough to build a feature or a product based on perliminary research.

Your prototypes will open the door to more questions

The prototype is essentially a research tool, because it's a means by which you communicate your idea to stakeholders.

Research and prototyping are powerful allies

When you combine initial research with prototyping and user testing, you'll get to an answer faster and ship your product with more certainty.

There are many methods of prototyping for the web, each best suited to eliciting a specific type of feedback.

Prototyping with sketches

Let's start with sketched prototypes.
Interface elements are loosely drawn with a focus on the big picture.

Create quick, disposable, sketches to communicate
your ideas

Talking about ideas is boring. Use sketches to better illustrate your concepts.

String your images together in a clickable prototype to test your concepts

Best for early concepts

Early is a little misleading. This can be early in an entirely new product or feature or as a means of communicating an early idea to solve a detailed problem.

Avoid details in your sketches

Don't attempt to work through detailed on-page interactions. Keep it at a high-level.

Get high-level feedback

Focus on the big picture. If someone asks about specific details— should the button should be on the right or the left side? Divert the conversation back to topic.

Prototyping with wireframes

Now we're starting to get a little higher in fidelity.
Wireframe prototypes are much more detailed. Your prototypes are now beginning to resemble actual screens.

Create detailed wireframes with content and hierarchy

Wireframes are great for working through detailed content and hierarchy

Test detailed user flows with a clickable prototype made using existing wireframes

Best while “in the weeds”

When you need to work through detailed flows and how your site behaves when the user takes certain actions.

Focus on the content

When you present this prototype, make sure to emphasize that visuals and layout are not important at this stage.

Get feedback on your flows

In addition to content and hierarchy, you're looking to identify gaps in your flow. Did you forget a step in the checkout? Did the user expect one thing but found something else?

Prototyping with code

Prototyping in code is exactly what it sounds like.
You can code up your prototypes from scratch, using a framework, or an existing styleguide.

Whoa

Prototyping in code is exactly what it sounds like.

Best for…???

Coded prototypes are tricky because the tools don't restrict fidelity this time. You can work at a wireframe or detailed visual mockup fidelity.

Best when you have an
existing styleguide

Best when you already have a styleguide and you can quickly use those existing components to create new flows.
This is the best way of minimzing time duplicating code for production. While creating an internal styleguide can seem daunting, the initial investment pays off big time when the team is working on features.

Focus on flows

Prototyping in code gives you the best flexibility to test interactions and flows, so seize this opportunity.

Know when to stop

Don't get caught up in code standards and functionality, at first. You can fake most functionality. The goal isn't to build the feature, or have the most immaculate code, but to simulate it for testing.
